d & f-Block Elements — Answer Key

Q1. OS of Mn in MnO₄⁻.

Answer: (D) +7

x + 4(−2) = −1 ⇒ x = +7. All 3d and 4s electrons removed (d⁰).

Q2. Electrons accepted by Cr₂O₇²⁻ in acidic medium.

Answer: (C) 6

Cr₂O₇²⁻ + 14H⁺ + 6e⁻ → 2Cr³⁺ + 7H₂O. Each Cr goes +6 → +3 (3 e⁻), two Cr atoms → 6 e⁻ total.

Q3. μ of Fe²⁺ high-spin (d⁶).

Answer: (C) 4.90 BM

d⁶ high-spin: t2g⁴ eg² → 4 unpaired. μ = √(4×6) = √24 = 4.90 BM.

Q4. Colourless pair.

Answer: (B) Sc³⁺ and Zn²⁺

Sc³⁺ is d⁰, Zn²⁺ is d¹⁰ — no d-d transition possible → colourless. All others have partially filled d orbitals.

Q5. Consequence of lanthanoid contraction.

Answer: (A) Similar chemistry of Zr and Hf

Lanthanoid contraction makes the radii of 5d (Hf) nearly equal to 4d (Zr) counterparts → very similar chemistry, difficult to separate.

Q6. Electronic config of Cr.

Answer: (B) [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹

Anomalous: a half-filled 3d⁵ plus half-filled 4s¹ is more stable than 3d⁴4s² due to exchange energy.

Q7. Lanthanoid with +4 OS.

Answer: (C) Cerium

Ce: [Xe]4f¹5d¹6s². Ce⁴⁺ becomes [Xe] (noble gas config) → very stable. Hence the classic +4 state. Ce⁴⁺ is a strong oxidising agent.

Q8. NOT a property of transition elements.

Answer: (D) Low melting and boiling points

Transition metals have high melting/boiling points due to strong metallic bonding involving d-electrons.

Q9. Interstitial compounds — nature.

Answer: (B) Hard, high melting, metallic

H, C, N enter gaps in metallic lattice → extra bonding → very hard, chemically inert, high MP, retain metallic conductivity. Examples: steel, TiC, WC.

Q10. Why actinoids show more OS than lanthanoids.

Answer: (B) 5f, 6d, 7s orbitals close in energy

In actinoids these orbitals overlap in energy, so more electrons participate in bonding → diverse oxidation states (+3 to +7).
