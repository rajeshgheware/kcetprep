Q1. Henry's law constant with temperature.

Answer: (A) Increases with temperature

Solubility of gases decreases as T rises (dissolution is exothermic). Since KH = p/x, lower x for same p means higher KH.

Q2. Vapour pressure after adding glucose.

Answer: (A) 23.56 mmHg

nwater = 180/18 = 10 mol; nglucose = 18/180 = 0.1 mol. xwater = 10/10.1 = 0.9901. P = 0.9901 × 23.8 = 23.56 mmHg.

Q3. Molality of 3 M glucose, density 1.2.

Answer: (C) 4.55 m

1 L soln = 1200 g. Mass glucose = 3 × 180 = 540 g. Mass water = 1200 − 540 = 660 g = 0.66 kg. m = 3 / 0.66 = 4.55 m.

Q4. Why camphor for Rast method.

Answer: (B) Very high Kf

High Kf gives a measurable ΔTf even for small amounts of solute, improving accuracy.

Q5. Osmotic pressure of 0.2 M sucrose at 300 K.

Answer: (C) 4.93 atm

π = CRT = 0.2 × 0.0821 × 300 = 4.926 atm. (Sucrose is a non-electrolyte, i = 1.)

Q6. i for fully dissociated K4[Fe(CN)6].

Answer: (D) 5

K4[Fe(CN)6] → 4 K+ + [Fe(CN)6]4− = 5 particles.

Q7. HCl–water boils higher than both.

Answer: (B) Maximum boiling azeotrope (negative deviation)

Higher BP than either component → stronger A–B interactions → negative deviation. HCl–water is a classic max-BP azeotrope.

Q8. Highest π among 0.1 M solutions.

Answer: (C) CaCl2

π ∝ iC. i values: urea/glucose = 1, NaCl = 2, CaCl2 = 3. So CaCl2 gives the highest π.

Q9. Acetone + chloroform negative deviation.

Answer: (B) H-bonding between acetone C=O and chloroform C–H strengthens A–B attraction

When A–B attractions exceed A–A and B–B, vapour pressure is lower than predicted → negative deviation and max BP azeotrope.

Q10. Mole fraction of solute in 1 molal aqueous.

Answer: (D) 0.0177

1 molal = 1 mol solute in 1 kg water. Moles water = 1000/18 = 55.5. xsolute = 1 / (1 + 55.5) = 0.0177.
