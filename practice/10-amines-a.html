Q1. Hofmann bromamide.

Answer: (A) R-CONH₂ → R-NH₂ (one C less)

Amide → primary amine with Br₂/NaOH. The carbonyl C is lost as CO₃²⁻. Note the carbon-count reduction.

Q2. Gabriel synthesis.

Answer: (A) Pure 1° aliphatic amines

Phthalimide-K⁺ + R-X (SN2) → N-alkylphthalimide → hydrolysis → R-NH₂. Can't work with aryl halides (no SN2).

Q3. Basicity order in water.

Answer: (C) 2° > 1° ≈ 3° > NH₃

Balance of +I effect (3° most donating) and H-bonding/hydration (3° least hydrated — no N-H). In gas phase pure +I wins: 3°>2°>1°. In water, 2° strikes best balance.

Q4. Hinsberg for 2° amine.

Answer: (B) Sulphonamide insoluble in NaOH

2° amine gives R₂N-SO₂-C₆H₅, no acidic N-H → not soluble in NaOH. 1° gives a soluble salt (acidic N-H). 3° doesn't react.

Q5. Carbylamine test.

Answer: (A) 1° amines

R-NH₂ + CHCl₃ + 3KOH → R-NC (isocyanide, foul smell) + 3KCl + 3H₂O. Diagnostic for 1° amines only.

Q6. Diazotisation temperature.

Answer: (B) 0–5 °C

Above 5 °C the diazonium salt decomposes to phenol + N₂. Cold conditions are essential.

Q7. Why aniline < methylamine (basicity).

Answer: (B) Lone pair delocalised into ring

Resonance of N lone pair into benzene ring lowers electron density on N → less available to bond H⁺.

Q8. Nitrobenzene + Sn/HCl.

Answer: (C) Aniline

Sn/HCl (or Fe/HCl, H₂/Ni) reduces -NO₂ to -NH₂ directly. Intermediate species exist but are not isolated.

Q9. CH₃CN + LiAlH₄.

Answer: (B) Ethylamine

Nitrile reduction adds 2H across C≡N → CH₃-CH₂-NH₂. Note: carbon count retained (unlike Hofmann).

Q10. Strongest base among anilines.

Answer: (C) p-Methoxyaniline

-OCH₃ is an EDG (+R effect), increases electron density on N, strengthens basicity. -NO₂ and -Cl are EW → weaker base.
