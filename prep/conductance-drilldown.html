1. The ladder of terms — know this cold

From resistance to Λ°m

R = ρ · (l/A)  ·  κ = 1/ρ  ·  G = 1/R
κ = G × (l/A) = G × G*
Λm = κ × 1000 / C (C in mol/L, κ in S cm⁻¹)
Λm = κ / c (c in mol/m³, κ in S m⁻¹)
Cell constant G* = l/A (units cm⁻¹ or m⁻¹). Fixed for a given conductivity cell.

Trap: If κ is in S cm⁻¹ and C in mol/L, use the factor ×1000. If κ is in S m⁻¹ and c is in mol/m³, use ÷c. Mixing conventions gives answers off by 10⁴.

2. Strong vs weak electrolytes — graph you must recognise

Λm vs √C

Strong: linear, small drop — extrapolate y-intercept → Λ°m. Weak: sharp rise near C=0; y-intercept not reachable from data → use Kohlrausch.

Why strong is linear, weak is curvy

Strong (fully dissociated): On dilution, ion–ion attraction ↓ → ions move more freely → Λm rises slowly and smoothly toward Λ°m.
Weak (partially dissociated): On dilution, α ↑ sharply (more molecules ionise) → Λm jumps up near infinite dilution.
Strong: Λm = Λ°m − A√C (Debye–Hückel–Onsager)
Trap: "On dilution κ decreases but Λm increases." Reason: κ is per cm³ of solution (fewer ions per volume), Λm is per mole (same moles, better mobility).

3. Kohlrausch's law — the plug-and-chug template

Statement

Λ°m(salt) = ν+ λ°+ + ν− λ°−
Limiting molar conductivity of any electrolyte = sum of individual ionic contributions (each weighted by its stoichiometric coefficient).
Ex. Λ°m(Al₂(SO₄)₃) = 2·λ°(Al³⁺) + 3·λ°(SO₄²⁻)
Why it matters: only way to get Λ°m of a weak electrolyte (can't extrapolate directly).

Template: Λ° of weak acid/base

Build the weak electrolyte as a linear combination of three strong electrolytes whose Λ°m are tabulated.
TargetRecipe
CH₃COOHCH₃COONa + HCl − NaCl
NH₄OHNH₄Cl + NaOH − NaCl

H₂O (for Kw)HCl + NaOH − NaCl
Trap: always subtract the "shared counter-ions" salt (usually NaCl). Students often add instead of subtract.

4. α and Ka from conductance

Degree of dissociation

α = Λm / Λ°m (weak electrolyte only)
At concentration C, measured Λm / limiting Λ°m gives the fraction that dissociated.
Trap: this only works for weak electrolytes. Strong electrolytes have α ≈ 1 at all dilutions, so the Λm/Λ°m ratio loses physical meaning as "α".

Ostwald's dilution law (Ka)

Ka = Cα² / (1 − α) ≈ Cα² (α ≪ 1)
Ka = C · (Λm/Λ°m)² / (1 − Λm/Λ°m)
Chain to remember: Λm (measured) → α = Λm/Λ°m → Ka = Cα²/(1−α).
For very weak acids (CH₃COOH, HCN) use the approximation Ka ≈ Cα² → α = √(Ka/C).

6. Traps that cost marks

Top 6 exam traps

  1. Unit mismatch: κ given in S m⁻¹ but C in mol/L → forgets 10⁻² factor.
  2. ×1000 factor: omitting it is the single most common mistake. Λm = κ × 1000 / C (not κ/C).
  3. Kohlrausch sign: for CH₃COOH, you subtract NaCl (not add). Net-ions trick: write each as ions and cancel spectators.
  4. α for strong electrolytes: ratio Λm/Λ°m ≠ α for strong; it reflects inter-ionic effects, not dissociation.
  5. Stoichiometry in Kohlrausch: for CaCl₂, Λ° = λ°(Ca²⁺) + 2·λ°(Cl⁻). Forgetting the 2 is a classic.
  6. On dilution: κ ↓ and Λm ↑ — direction is opposite. Remember "κ per volume, Λ per mole".
