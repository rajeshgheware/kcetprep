ALCOHOLS, PHENOLS & ETHERS24 Q / 8 yrs · 3.0 per paper

Preparation — four reliable routes

Grignard patterns: HCHO → 1° alcohol; R'CHO → 2° alcohol; R'₂CO → 3° alcohol; CO₂ → carboxylic acid (1 C more).
Phenol prep: (a) Cumene process — cumene + O₂ → cumene hydroperoxide → phenol + acetone (industrial); (b) Dow — chlorobenzene + NaOH @ 350 °C, 150 atm; (c) from diazonium — ArN₂⁺ + H₂O, Δ.
Ether prep: Williamson — R–ONa + R'–X → R–O–R' (SN2, use 1° R'X).
LiAlH₄ reduces everything (ald/ket/COOH/ester/amide/nitrile). NaBH₄ only ald/ket. Reagent choice matters in KCET questions.

Lucas test — distinguishing 1°, 2°, 3° alcohols

Reagent: conc. HCl + anhydrous ZnCl₂ (Lucas reagent) at room temperature
Why it works: the reaction produces R–Cl which is insoluble in the aqueous reagent → turbidity. Faster = more stable carbocation intermediate.
Reactivity with HX overall: 3° > 2° > 1° > CH₃OH. And HI > HBr > HCl (bond strength).
Tertiary alcohols + HCl need no ZnCl₂ (they go by SN1 anyway). The ZnCl₂ "activates" 1°/2° for SN1-like kick.

Oxidation behaviour — 1° vs 2° vs 3°

PCC (pyridinium chlorochromate) is selective — stops 1° alcohol at aldehyde. Strong oxidants (KMnO₄, K₂Cr₂O₇) push to COOH.
Dehydration: R–OH + conc. H₂SO₄ / Δ → alkene (Zaitsev). Ease: 3° > 2° > 1°.
Oppenauer oxidation: 2° alcohol + Al-tert-butoxide → ketone (selective, no over-oxidation).
3° alcohols resist oxidation because there is no α-H on the carbinol C. This is a classic distinguishing test.

Phenol — acidity & electrophilic substitution

Reimer-Tiemann: PhOH + CHCl₃ + NaOH → o-HO–C₆H₄–CHO (salicylaldehyde)
Kolbe: PhO⁻Na⁺ + CO₂ (Δ, P) → o-HO–C₆H₄–COOH (salicylic → aspirin)
Acidity boosters (EWG on phenol): p-nitrophenol > m-nitrophenol > phenol > p-cresol. Picric acid (2,4,6-trinitrophenol) is very strong.
Bromination: in H₂O (polar) → 2,4,6-tribromophenol (white ppt, fast). In CS₂ (non-polar) → mostly p-bromophenol (monobromo).
Phenol: FeCl₃ test → violet colour (enol-like complex). Distinguishes phenols from alcohols.

Ethers — cleavage with HI

Why different? Aryl C–O bond has partial double-bond character (resonance) → very strong, unbreakable by SN. So cleavage happens at the alkyl C–O side.
Order of reactivity: HI > HBr > HCl (I⁻ is best Nu).
Williamson limits: SN2 on 1° halide only. 3° halide + alkoxide → elimination (alkene), not ether.
Anisole + HI → phenol + methyl iodide. Not methanol + phenyl iodide — aryl C–O survives.

Allyl / benzyl halides: fast by both SN1 and SN2 (resonance-stabilised carbocation AND less hindered).
Vinyl / aryl halides: neither SN1 nor SN2 at normal conditions — C–X bond has partial double-bond character, sp² C, no back-side attack.
Rule of thumb: 1° methyl halide + strong Nu in acetone → SN2. 3° halide + weak Nu in water → SN1. Work backwards from conditions.

Elimination vs substitution — when does E win?

Saytzeff: more substituted alkene is major product (thermodynamic — more hyperconjugation).
Hofmann: with very bulky base (t-BuO⁻, NH₂⁻) or quaternary ammonium → less-substituted alkene is major.
Aq. KOH vs alc. KOH: aqueous does substitution (R–X → R–OH); alcoholic does elimination (R–X → alkene). Classic KCET question.
E1 vs E2: E1 uses carbocation (parallel to SN1). E2 is one-step with anti-periplanar H and X.

Haloarenes — why they don't do ordinary SN

Three reasons Ar–X resists nucleophile: (1) C–X partial double-bond (resonance) → strong bond; (2) sp² C is flat, no back-side attack; (3) aromaticity is lost in SN1 cation.
When Ar–X does react: strong EWG at ortho/para stabilises the Meisenheimer intermediate. e.g., 2,4-DNP, 2,4,6-TNB react with OH⁻ to form picric acid.
Dow process: chlorobenzene + 15% NaOH / 350 °C / 150 atm → phenol. Only with extreme conditions.
Wurtz: 2 R–X + 2 Na → R–R (alkane, even C). Wurtz-Fittig: ArX + RX + Na → Ar–R. Fittig: 2 ArX + 2 Na → Ar–Ar.

EQUILIBRIUM14 Q / 8 yrs · 1.75 per paper (Support 5)

Kc, Kp, Kx — the three equilibrium constants

aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD
K_c = [C]^c[D]^d / [A]^a[B]^b     K_p = p_C^c p_D^d / p_A^a p_B^b
K_p = K_c · (RT)^Δn   where Δn = (c+d) − (a+b)
Units: K_c has units (mol/L)^Δn. K_p has units (atm)^Δn. Dimensionless only when Δn = 0.
When Δn = 0: K_p = K_c (e.g., H₂ + I₂ ⇌ 2HI).
K_x (mole-fraction form): K_p = K_x · P^Δn — pressure changes shift composition if Δn ≠ 0.
Pure solids and pure liquids do not appear in K expressions (activity = 1). Water in aqueous equilibria is also omitted.

Le Chatelier — predict the shift

Catalyst: does NOT shift equilibrium — only speeds up both forward and reverse equally (reaches equilibrium faster).
Inert gas at constant V: no effect (partial pressures of reacting species don't change).
Inert gas at constant P: total V increases → effectively reduces partial pressures → shift to side with more moles of gas.
Temperature is the only factor that changes K itself. Concentration/pressure changes shift position but K stays the same.

Ionic equilibrium — pH & Kw

K_w = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ at 25 °C
pH = −log[H⁺]   pOH = −log[OH⁻]   pH + pOH = 14
Strong acid/base: fully dissociate. [H⁺] = C (acid conc). pH = −log C.
Weak acid: HA ⇌ H⁺ + A⁻. K_a = Cα² (when α small). [H⁺] = √(K_a · C). pH = ½ (pK_a − log C).
Weak base: [OH⁻] = √(K_b · C). pOH = ½ (pK_b − log C).
K_w relation: K_a × K_b = K_w for conjugate pair. pK_a + pK_b = 14.
Kw varies with T: increases with temperature (water dissociation is endothermic). At 50 °C, Kw ≈ 5×10⁻¹⁴; pH of pure water < 7 but still neutral.

Buffers — Henderson-Hasselbalch

Examples: CH₃COOH + CH₃COONa (acidic, pH ≈ pKa = 4.76 when [A⁻]=[HA]); NH₃ + NH₄Cl (basic, pOH ≈ pKb).
Buffer capacity is maximum when [A⁻] = [HA] → pH = pKa. Effective buffer range: pKa ± 1.
Adding small acid/base: the conjugate pair absorbs it. H⁺ + A⁻ → HA; OH⁻ + HA → A⁻ + H₂O. pH barely changes.
Blood is buffered primarily by H₂CO₃ / HCO₃⁻ (pKa ~6.1), kept at pH 7.4. Deviations cause acidosis/alkalosis.

Solubility product & hydrolysis

For A_xB_y(s) ⇌ xA⁺ + yB⁻   K_sp = [A⁺]ˣ [B⁻]ʸ
Ionic product (Q): Q < K_sp → unsaturated (more can dissolve); Q = K_sp → saturated; Q > K_sp → precipitates.
Common ion effect: adding a shared ion suppresses dissociation. AgCl in NaCl: [Ag⁺] = K_sp / [Cl⁻] → lower solubility.
Salt hydrolysis:
• Strong acid + strong base → neutral (NaCl, KNO₃).
• Strong acid + weak base → acidic (NH₄Cl, CuSO₄).
• Weak acid + strong base → basic (CH₃COONa, Na₂CO₃).
• Weak acid + weak base → depends on Ka vs Kb.
Degree of hydrolysis h = √(K_w / (K_a · C)) for salt of weak acid + strong base. pH = 7 + ½(pKa + log C).
