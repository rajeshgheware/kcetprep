ALDEHYDES, KETONES & CARBOXYLIC ACIDS28 Q / 8 yrs · 3.5 per paper

Preparation — the five routes you must know

Etard: toluene + CrO₂Cl₂ → benzaldehyde (stops at CHO, no over-oxidation).
Ozonolysis: alkene + O₃ / Zn–H₂O → 2 carbonyls. Double bond splits, each carbon gets =O.
From alkyne: HC≡CH + H₂O (HgSO₄) → CH₃CHO (Markovnikov). Internal alkyne → ketone.
Rosenmund = acid chloride → aldehyde (stops there, thanks to BaSO₄ poisoning). Stephen = nitrile → aldehyde.

Nucleophilic addition — why aldehydes > ketones

Nu⁻ attacks Cδ⁺ of C=O → tetrahedral alkoxide → protonation → alcohol/adduct
Standard Nu: HCN (cyanohydrin), NaHSO₃ (bisulphite, ald + methyl ketones only), RMgX (Grignard → alcohol), R–OH/H⁺ (hemiacetal → acetal), R–NH₂ (imine / Schiff base, loses H₂O).
Reactivity order: HCHO > RCHO > ArCHO > R₂CO > R–Ar–CO > Ar₂CO.
NaHSO₃ test works only for ald + methyl ketones (steric). Used to purify carbonyls.

α-H reactions: Aldol vs Cannizzaro

Crossed Cannizzaro: HCHO + ArCHO → ArCH₂OH + HCOO⁻ (HCHO always oxidised).
Aldol candidates: CH₃CHO ✓, CH₃COCH₃ ✓, C₆H₅CHO ✗, HCHO ✗, (CH₃)₃C–CHO ✗, C₆H₅–CO–C₆H₅ ✗.
No α-H → Cannizzaro. Has α-H → Aldol. Test for α-H = look for H on the carbon next to C=O.

Named reactions — reduction, α-halo, Clemmensen, WK

C=O —[Zn(Hg), conc. HCl]→ CH₂  Clemmensen
C=O —[NH₂NH₂, KOH/ethylene glycol, Δ]→ CH₂  Wolff–Kishner
R–CH₂–COOH —[X₂, red P]→ R–CHX–COOH  Hell–Volhard–Zelinsky
Clemmensen = acid medium (use with acid-stable substrates). Wolff-Kishner = base medium (use with base-stable substrates). Both: C=O → CH₂.
HVZ works only on α-H of carboxylic acids; needs red P catalyst to form acid bromide intermediate.
Tollens & Fehling don't oxidise ketones — ketones lack the H on carbonyl C needed for oxidation to COOH.
If a KCET Q gives conditions "Zn–Hg/HCl" → think Clemmensen. "NH₂NH₂/KOH" → think WK. Both convert C=O to CH₂.

Carboxylic acid acidity & derivatives

EWG (–NO₂, –X, –CN) ↑ acidity by stabilising –COO⁻. EDG (–CH₃, –OCH₃, –NH₂) ↓ acidity.
Aromatic: p-NO₂–C₆H₄–COOH > benzoic > p-CH₃–C₆H₄–COOH. ortho-effect: o-substituents often ↑ acidity regardless of nature (steric/hydrogen bonding).
R–COOH → R–COCl (SOCl₂) → R–COO–COR (dehydrate) → R–COOR' (ROH/H⁺) → R–CONH₂ (NH₃, Δ)
pKa ranking: HCOOH (3.77) < CH₃COOH (4.76). HCOOH is stronger — no EDG methyl. Also gives positive Tollens — it has a C–H bond.
AMINES24 Q / 8 yrs · 3.0 per paper

Preparation — three reliable routes

1° only, no aryl Nitro Ar–NO₂ [Sn/HCl] → Ar–NH₂
Hoffmann bromamide: R–CONH₂ + Br₂ + 4 NaOH → R–NH₂ + Na₂CO₃ + 2 NaBr + 2 H₂O. One carbon is lost.
Gabriel synthesis: phthalimide (–NH) → K salt → R–X alkylation → hydrolysis → pure 1° R–NH₂. Does not work for aryl amines (no SN on aryl halides).
Ammonolysis: R–X + NH₃ (excess) → gives mixture 1°/2°/3°/4°. Not preferred for pure amine.
Hoffmann vs Stephen vs Rosenmund: Hoffmann (amide→amine, 1 C less), Stephen (nitrile→aldehyde), Rosenmund (acid chloride→aldehyde). Easy to confuse.

Basicity — gas phase vs aqueous

Aqueous anomaly: 2° > 1° > 3°. Three factors compete: +I (more R ↑ basic), solvation of R₃NH⁺ (fewer H ↓ solvation ↓ basic), steric around N (more R ↓ basic).
Aromatic: aniline is far less basic than NH₃ — lone pair delocalised into ring. EDG (–OCH₃, –CH₃, –NH₂) at p-position ↑ basicity; EWG (–NO₂, –CN) ↓ basicity sharply.
Order: p-toluidine > aniline > p-nitroaniline. m-NO₂-aniline > p-NO₂-aniline (resonance only at o,p; meta acts via –I only).
Methylamine > NH₃ > aniline. Aliphatic > ammonia > aromatic. Memorise this one spine.

Hinsberg logic: 1° has two H on N (one replaced by SO₂Ph, the other is acidic → NaOH-soluble). 2° has one H (replaced by SO₂Ph → neutral, NaOH-insoluble). 3° has zero H → no reaction.
Carbylamine is exclusive to 1° amines. It's also used to identify primary amines. Smell = rotten/intolerable.

Diazonium salts — the Sandmeyer hub

Ar–NH₂ + NaNO₂ + 2 HCl —[0–5 °C]→ Ar–N₂⁺Cl⁻ + NaCl + 2 H₂O
Must be cold: < 5 °C or the diazonium decomposes (Ar–N₂⁺ → Ar–OH + N₂).
Why so useful: –N₂⁺ is a "leaving group" that opens aryl substitution where halide SN fails. Planning routes to aryl halides, phenols, nitriles, fluorides goes through diazonium.
Gatterman uses Cu/HCl (Sandmeyer uses CuCl). I⁻ and F⁻ routes skip Cu (direct KI and HBF₄).

Aniline — electrophilic substitution & N-protection

–NH₂ is a strong o,p-director + activator. But in strong acid it protonates to –NH₃⁺, which is meta-directing and deactivating. Acetylation (→ –NHCOCH₃) tones down the lone pair and avoids protonation.
Bromination without protection: aniline + Br₂/H₂O → 2,4,6-tribromoaniline (instant white ppt). Used as a test.
Anilinium ion = meta-director. Acetanilide = mild o,p-director (para selectivity). This trick (protect → react → deprotect) is a classic KCET Q.
BIOMOLECULES22 Q / 8 yrs · 2.75 per paper

Glucose — open chain & pyranose ring

Mutarotation: solid α-glucose (+112°) and β-glucose (+19°) interconvert in water via open chain → equilibrium optical rotation +52.5°.
Glucose reactions: HI/Δ → n-hexane (proves straight chain); HCN → cyanohydrin · NH₂OH → oxime (both confirm a free –CHO); Tollens +ve; Fehling +ve (reducing sugar).
What's missing? Glucose does not give 2,4-DNP/Schiff tests (exists mostly as cyclic hemiacetal, free –CHO < 0.1%).
Fructose is a ketose but still reducing (isomerises to glucose in alkaline Tollens/Fehling medium).

Amino acids — zwitterion & classification

Isoelectric point (pI) — pH at which the amino acid is a neutral zwitterion (no migration in electric field).
Classification: Neutral (Gly, Ala, Val, Leu, Ile, Phe, ...), Acidic (Asp, Glu — extra –COOH), Basic (Lys, Arg, His — extra –NH₂/guanidinium/imidazole).
Essential (must-eat) amino acids: Val, Leu, Ile, Thr, Lys, Met, Phe, Trp (+ His, Arg in children). Mnemonic: "Private Tim Hall" or "VIL TLiMPh-Trp".
Peptide bond: –CO–NH– formed between –COOH of one and –NH₂ of next; water is lost.
At pH < pI amino acid is cationic (moves to cathode); pH > pI anionic (moves to anode); pH = pI no net motion.

Protein structure — four levels

Fibrous proteins (keratin, collagen, myosin): long sheets/helices, insoluble, structural.
Globular proteins (haemoglobin, insulin, enzymes): folded compact, water-soluble, functional.
Denaturation: loss of 2°/3°/4° structure (heat, pH, urea, alcohol) while 1° stays intact. Egg-white cooking, milk curdling.
Only primary structure is defined by covalent (peptide) bonds. All higher levels are non-covalent except for disulphide bridges in tertiary.

Nucleic acids — DNA vs RNA

T double helix (Watson-Crick) A=T (2 H-bonds), G≡C (3) RNA sugar: ribose (2'-OH) bases: A, G, C, U single strand mRNA, tRNA, rRNA
Nucleoside = base + sugar. Nucleotide = base + sugar + phosphate. Nucleic acid = polymer of nucleotides via phosphodiester bonds (3'-5').
Purines (two rings): A, G. Pyrimidines (one ring): C, T, U. Chargaff: %A = %T, %G = %C in DNA.
Roles: DNA stores genetic info; mRNA carries code to ribosome; tRNA brings amino acids; rRNA is part of ribosome.
Memorise: DNA has T and deoxyribose; RNA has U and ribose (with 2'-OH). Base pairing H-bonds: A–T = 2, G–C = 3.
